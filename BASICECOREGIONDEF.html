Pineywoods

The East Texas region is mostly composed of a thick forest of pine trees. This large forest is part of a much larger "region of trees" that extends into many neighboring states! The terrain is hilly with low, wet bottomlands that grow a variety hardwood trees such as elm, mesquite and ash. This region is home to a variety of plants and animals whose habitat preferences are woodlands and shorelines. Among them are: cottonmouth snakes, squirrels, rabbits and opossums. Gulf Praries and Marshes

The Gulf Coastal Plains and Marshes Ecoregion is Texas's main coastal ecoregion! It is vaguely defined as being inside a coastal band from the state boundary of Lousiana to the city of Brownsville 61 miles wide. The Gulf Coastal Plains ecoregion of Texas spans the coastal plain and lowlands of the Gulf of Mexico. It features a mild climate with high humidity and ample rainfall, as well as distinctive wetlands that provide critical habitat for a variety of species and support the ecosystem's health. The region's plant communities include coastal prairies, pine forests, and hardwood forests, while its animal species range from numerous bird species to fish and amphibians. Threats to the ecosystem include habitat loss and fragmentation, pollution, and climate change. Conservation efforts are underway to protect the region and preserve its biodiversity.

Post Oak Savanna

The Post Oak Savanna ecoregion in Texas is a flat to gently rolling area that is dominated by the post oak tree. Its open grasslands, scattered trees, and fertile soil support a diverse array of plant and animal species. The region is home to mammals like white-tailed deer, birds such as the northern bobwhite, and pollinators like bees and butterflies. The Post Oak Savanna has a history of human habitation and use, with indigenous tribes and European settlers using the region for hunting, ranching, and agriculture. Blackland Prarie

The Texas Blackland Prairies ecoregion is a temperate grasslands, savannas, and shrublands ecoregion located in the central part of Texas, United States. It covers an area of approximately 17 million acres and is characterized by its deep, fertile soils that support tallgrass prairie vegetation. The region has a humid subtropical climate with hot summers and mild winters. Historically, the Blackland Prairies were home to large herds of bison and supported diverse wildlife populations. Today, much of the land has been converted to agriculture or urban development, but there are still some areas that remain relatively intact and provide important habitat for native species.

Crosstimbers

The Cross Timbers ecoregion is a region of mixed-grass prairie and forest in central North America, primarily in Texas, Oklahoma, and Kansas. It is located between the Great Plains to the west and the eastern deciduous forests to the east. The region is characterized by its unique blend of tallgrass prairie grasses and hardwood trees such as oak and hickory. The main industry of the Cross-Timbers Ecoregion is farming and there are no major metropolitan areas in the region. It is known as one of the least altered texas Ecoregions beacuse of the long lasting thick overgrowth.

South Texas Plains

The South Texas plains is a biologically diverse ecoregion that streteches from the Southeast reached of the Texas Hill Country into the subtropical regions of the Lower Rio Grande Valley. The region is dotted with lakes, many of them oxbow lakes, which are a large contributor to the region's biodiversity. The Rio Grande also flows through the region. It has a average to slightly hot climate and moderate rainfall(about20-30cm a year). Major cities in this ecoregion are Alice and Guadalupe.

Edward's Plateau

The Edwards Plateau in central Texas is a visually stunning and ecologically diverse region. Its rugged terrain, limestone hills, and deep canyons create a dramatic landscape. The Edwards Aquifer, an underground water source, supports numerous springs and sustains a thriving ecosystem. Diverse habitats like oak woodlands and grasslands are home to a variety of plant and animal species, including endangered ones. The area also holds cultural and historical significance with evidence of Native American settlements and European exploration. The Edwards Plateau offers a captivating and enriching experience in its natural beauty and recreational opportunities.

Rolling Plains

The Rolling Plains ecoregion in West Texas showcases gently rolling hills, expansive grasslands, and scattered mesquite woodlands. Its semi-arid climate features hot summers and cool winters, with irregular rainfall averaging 20-30 inches annually. The grasslands support grazing animals like bison and deer, while the woodlands provide habitat for various mammals, birds, and reptiles. Additionally, the Rolling Plains serves as a crucial migratory corridor for numerous bird species.

High Plains

The High Plains ecoregion in Texas features vast stretches of flat or gently rolling terrain and encompasses a semi-arid climate. It is known for its expansive grasslands and agricultural significance. The region experiences hot summers and cold winters, with limited precipitation. It supports diverse plant and animal species adapted to the arid conditions, including prairie grasses, cacti, and species like pronghorn antelope and migratory birds.

Trans-Pecos

The Trans-Pecos ecoregion in Texas is characterized by its rugged and mountainous terrain. Located in the western part of the state, it encompasses the Chihuahuan Desert and the Davis Mountains. T he climate is arid, with hot summers and cool winters, and the region receives limited rainfall. The Trans-Pecos supports a unique array of plant and animal species adapted to desert conditions, including various cacti, yuccas, and desert-dwelling mammals like roadrunners and desert bighorn sheep. The ecoregion is also home to diverse ecosystems, including desert scrublands, grasslands, and riparian areas along rivers and springs.
